## What the Complaint Asks For

The complaint asks for unified regulatory action across the agencies and the legislative branches to require platform disclosure of operator-payment streams that fund the consensus-engineering apparatus, disclosure of foreign engagement-cluster labor performing American-citizen-identity in coordinated paid campaigns, algorithmic transparency at the differential-visibility level where platforms gate citizen content for commercial reasons, restoration of public API access that allows independent verification of platform claims, and coordination across regulatory authorities so the platforms cannot deploy jurisdictional-fragmentation defenses against the broader inquiry.

The complaint does not ask for the platforms to be shut down. The complaint does not ask for foreign voices to be excluded from American electronic public space — transparent foreign voices contribute legitimately to American discourse. The complaint is not anti-business. The platforms that adapt to transparency continue operating. The platforms that cannot survive transparency were not operating legitimate businesses to begin with.

The legal frameworks that reach this architecture already exist: the Federal Trade Commission Act of 1914, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the Foreign Agents Registration Act of 1938, the Federal Election Campaign Act, the Sherman and Clayton Acts, the Communications Act and the FCC's general jurisdiction, the state consumer-protection and public-nuisance authorities. What is missing is the unified will to apply them. That is what the complaint asks the receiving institutions to provide.
